Air Quality in Five Chinese Cities
By DANIEL OVERSTREET Nov. 18th, 2016
This paper has published several recent articles on air quality in China, which is measured by recording the concentration of PM2.5 in the air. PM2.5, or particulate matter less than 2.5 micrometers in diameter, is hazardous to your health because it can be inhaled into the lungs, where it causes respiratory problems. The less of it in the air, the better. Mark Zuckerberg's Smog Jog this March in Beijing occurred on a day classified as Unhealthy, which comes with the warning: "Active children and adults, and people with respiratory disease, such as asthma, should avoid prolonged outdoor exertion; everyone else, especially children, should limit prolonged outdoor exertion." PM2.5 readings are taken hourly. Each day's readings are then averaged and the result is classified as either: Good, Moderate, Unhealthy for sensitive groups, Unhealthy, Very Unhealthy, or Hazardous. Note that these daily averages overlook the fact that at certain points in a day, even a Good or Moderate day, a PM2.5 hourly reading can still be high and it is the rest of the day's readings that bring the daily average down into a safer category.
At the United States Embassy in Beijing, as well as Consulates in Chengdu, Guangzhou, Shanghai, and Shenyang, PM2.5 concentrations have been recorded every hour for the past several years, with recordings in Beijing beginning in 2008.
